Twelve agents and an empty directory is how a founder OS gets uninstalled on day
one. So is a full one. A founder who answers four rounds of questions and
receives a populated directory has been handed a filing cabinet: they did the
work, we did the filing, and nothing about tomorrow morning has changed. The
four steps below are not the product. They are the interview that pays for it.

**Onboarding ends by running the first `daily-brief` for real, on the state
these four steps just collected.** Not a summary of what was written — the
brief: the one thing that matters tomorrow, what is already rotting, and what
doing the one thing costs.

## Why the brief

It is the only thing this package can hand back on day one that the founder
could not have produced themselves in the same twenty minutes.

- **It needs nothing we do not already have.** Bets came from step 3, numbers
  from step 4, the client they would not take again from step 2. Nothing to
  fetch, nobody to wait for — ninety seconds after the last answer.
- **It hands back the one thing they did not do the work for: the ranking.**
  They supplied bets and numbers. The brief supplies a decision — which single
  thing tomorrow serves a bet, and what it costs. Every other candidate ending
  hands back their own inputs in a nicer font.
- **It is the recurring surface, met once.** `daily-brief` is what this package
  *is* on a normal Tuesday. End onboarding anywhere else and the founder's first
  brief arrives unannounced at 07:00 tomorrow from a cadence they have never
  seen, and reads as a notification. End here and the 07:00 one is their second.
- **It leaves the machine running rather than primed.** The brief writes its one
  thing into `queue.md` `## Doing` with an id, and tomorrow's brief opens by
  asking whether that id closed. Onboarding therefore ends mid-loop, with a
  question outstanding that has the founder's name on it. That is a reason to
  come back. A populated directory has never been one.

Rejected: a workspace summary — a receipt for their own labour. A strategy memo
— advice on twenty-minute-old state, which is house rule 2 run backwards.

Kept, from `founder-os-init`: `decisions/YYYY-MM-DD-founder-os-installed.md`.
One entry, written on day one, costing nothing — and it is what makes
`annual-review` a comparison rather than a memory twelve months from now. The
habit only exists if it starts before anyone feels like starting it.

## The order

Strict. Each step is worthless without the one before it, and the brief is
worthless without all four.

1. `write-charter` — who you are, what business, what "won" looks like. Runs
   `founder-os-init`: scaffolds the workspace, sets your timezone on the
   cadences, and begins without asking whether you are ready.
2. `define-icp` — who you serve and what you sell them
3. `set-quarter-goals` — the bets, with kill conditions
4. `baseline-metrics` — the numbers as they are today, not as hoped
5. **The brief.** At the end of step 4 the CFO hands to the **Chief of Staff** by
   name, and `daily-brief` runs on what steps 1–4 just wrote.

Nothing else in the company gives good advice until the first four exist. House
rule 1: no advice without state. An agent asked to help before onboarding will
guess, and guessing is the thing the founder can already do for free.

These tasks are deliberately unscheduled. You run them once, in order, now.

## What the first brief can and cannot see

`pipeline.md`, `clients/` and `reviews/daily/` are empty on day one. The first
brief has no yesterday to score and no overdue next action to find. It says so,
in one line, rather than filling the gap.

What it has is enough: bets from step 3, cash and burn and collected revenue
from step 4, and the client from step 2 the founder said they would not take
again. Step 2 wrote that client into `offer.md` `## ICP` `### Not this`, which
is one of the brief's inputs and is in its rotting definition for this exact
reason — if they are still on the books, that is day one's rotting item and the
founder did not have to look it up.

If the cash number came back unknown, that is day one's **one thing**, and it
outranks everything else in the file. `founder-os-init` files it straight into
`queue.md` `## Doing` rather than `## Queued`, because the brief reads `## Doing`
and is forbidden from reciting `## Queued` — and because it is not something the
founder might get to in three weeks. A company of one that cannot say its cash
number has exactly one job tomorrow.

A first brief that invents a pipeline to look impressive is the laundering house
rule 2 exists to stop, and it would be caught by the one person who knows — on
the single day they are still deciding whether to trust this. Thin and true
beats full and flattering.

## Never end on homework

No step here closes with "go and do X". A step that produces work for the founder
hands it to the **Chief of Staff**, and it lands in `queue.md` with an id, a bet
and a 21-day clock — see the `queue` skill. Spoken homework is not an obligation,
it is a thing the founder now feels bad about: no id, no owner, no expiry, and no
cadence will ever ask about it again.

That includes the unknowns. "Find your cash number" said out loud is homework.
`[q-MMDDx] find the cash number — bet: none` is an item the queue will chase, the
brief can name, and Friday's sweep will drop with a reason if it turns out nobody
meant it.

**Where it lands is the whole of whether the brief can name it.** The cash
unknown goes in `## Doing` — the brief reads that section and opens tomorrow by
asking whether it closed. Every other unanswered question goes in `## Queued`,
where its 21-day clock runs and Friday's sweep finds it. A cash unknown filed to
`## Queued` is an item the brief is explicitly forbidden to read out, which would
make it homework again — with an id, which is worse, because the id makes it look
handled.
