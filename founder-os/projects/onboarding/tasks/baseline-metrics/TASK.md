Run `revenue-review` against today. The numbers as they are, not as hoped:
revenue, collected versus invoiced, effective rate, runway.

This is the baseline every later claim gets measured against. House rule 2:
evidence over vibes — and this task is where the evidence starts.

## Then hand to the Chief of Staff, and have them run `daily-brief`

By name, explicitly, as the last thing this task does. That brief is the end of
onboarding and it is the point of onboarding.

Four steps in, you have told this company its business, its ICP, its bets and its
numbers, and so far you have received a directory. A directory is a filing
cabinet. Nobody opens a filing cabinet on day two. The brief is the first thing
that travels in the other direction: one thing that matters tomorrow, tied to a
bet you set twenty minutes ago; what is already rotting, in days; what doing the
one thing costs. Ninety seconds, entirely on your own state, none of which
anybody had to go and fetch.

You do not run it yourself. `reviews/daily/` belongs to the Chief of Staff —
house rule 4 — and the numbers you just closed are that brief's input, not the
brief.

The first one is thin in a specific, honest way: `pipeline.md` and `clients/` are
empty, so there is no overdue next action to find and no yesterday to score, and
the brief says that rather than inventing either. What it does have is enough. If
your cash number came back unknown, that is what is rotting and it will outrank
everything else in the file.

Anything this step leaves you owing — an invoice to chase, a number to dig out of
a bank export — is an item in `queue.md` with an id, not a sentence you are meant
to remember.
